Small Obsessions

I have a deep fascination for the USSR / Soviet world, but not in the blind nostalgic way people often imagine when they hear that sentence.

I am not interested in pretending history was clean. I know that political systems can crush, silence, deform and betray the very ideals they claim to defend. I know the difference between admiring a cultural force and denying its shadows.

What moves me is something else.

The architecture. The space program. The engineering. The posters. The cinema. The public art. The industrial design. The music. The scientific ambition. The strange beauty of objects made for a collective life.

There was a genius there that did not belong only to institutions. It belonged to people.

Engineers, workers, artists, teachers, scientists, mechanics, writers, designers, cosmonauts, anonymous citizens who built, repaired, imagined, transmitted and endured.

That is what I admire.

Not a perfect state. Not a clean myth. Not a flag used as a shortcut for thinking.

The human brilliance that survived inside the machine.

I also like old interfaces, strange systems, music tools, image experiments, obsolete machines, industrial objects and software that feels like it was made by a human being with fingerprints.

I like when an interface has a shape.

I like buttons that look like they could physically resist you. I like old operating systems, early graphical interfaces, technical labels, weird editors, experimental music software, badly documented tools, broken layouts, small local utilities and machines that seem to have survived something.

I do not really trust software that looks too perfect. Perfect surfaces often hide terrible rooms.

Boris
